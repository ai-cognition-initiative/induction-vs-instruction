# Set 1
You have 15 question sets × 1 draw at T=1 per cell. You also have deterministic seeds, so the question sets are shared across conditions and models. You can't separate within-set from between-set variance from a single draw per set. But you can get indirect evidence.
## The cross-condition consistency test.

At a given N near the transition, look at whether the same question sets tend to produce T across multiple conditions. Take your 15 seeds at, say, N=10 for a given model. For each seed, you have a binary outcome (T or P) in each of your 5 static conditions. Compute the per-seed sum: how many conditions produced T? If question content matters, some seeds will score 5/5 and others 0/5. If it's pure sampling noise, the scores should be roughly binomial around the mean.
You can test this with a chi-squared test or by computing the intraclass correlation across conditions. If the ICC is near zero, question content doesn't matter — all your variance is stochastic and you should invest in more draws per question set (or just use T=0). If the ICC is substantial, question content is a real factor and you need more question sets to characterize it.
##  The cross-model version of the same test.

 For a given condition and N, do the same seeds tend to produce T across different models? If seed #7 produces T on Gemma, Llama, and Claude, there's something about those questions that resists induction pressure regardless of model. That would be a strong finding and would justify investing compute in understanding what about those questions matters.

# Set 2
Here's what to check in your existing data, requiring no new compute:
Check 1: Is there cross-condition agreement per seed? At each transition-adjacent N, for each of the 15 seeds, count how many of the 5 static conditions produced T. If question content matters, the distribution of these counts should be overdispersed relative to what you'd expect from independent coin flips. Concretely: if the overall T rate at this N is 0.5, independent flips would give you a binomial(5, 0.5) distribution over the counts — most seeds scoring 2 or 3, few scoring 0 or 5. If you see many seeds at 0 or 5, question content is driving behavior.
Check 2: Is there cross-model agreement per seed? Same logic but across models instead of conditions. Pick 3-4 models that have similar overall T rates at a given N (so you're not just picking up on model difficulty differences). For each seed, count how many models produced T. Same overdispersion test.
Check 3: Are there "always T" or "always P" seeds? Even simpler — across all your conditions and models at a transition-adjacent N, are any of the 15 seeds unanimously T or unanimously P? Even one such seed, at an N where the overall rate is around 50%, is strong evidence that question content matters. With 5 conditions × say 5 models with similar transition points, that's 25 independent flips per seed. A seed that goes 25/25 T when the base rate is 0.5 has a probability of about 3 × 10⁻⁸ under the null of no question effect.
If these checks come back positive: replications of the 15 sets are well-justified. You'll be able to estimate per-set P(output=T), compute between-set variance, and identify which specific question sets anchor instruction-following. Three to four additional replications (giving you 4-5 draws per set) would be sufficient for rough per-set estimates.
If these checks come back negative: question content is not a meaningful factor, and additional T=1 replications of the same 15 sets just give you a tighter estimate of a single number. Still useful — you'd get a more precise transition curve — but less interesting analytically, and you'd know that investing in more question sets (or T=0 over many seeds) is the better path.
