\subsection{The transition is universal but model-dependent}
\label{sec:transition}

Every model we tested eventually shows greatly reduced instruction-following as the number of hardcoded turns~$N$ increases, in at least some of the experimental conditions, confirming that induction pressure is a universal phenomenon. However, the transition point varies considerably across models (Table~\ref{tab:results}; Figure ~\ref{fig:heatmaps}; individual model results in Appendix~\ref{app:permodel}). In fixed-output conditions, average instruction-following (IF) rates across all $N$ values range from 0.99 (Llama~3.3~70B) to 0.01 (Qwen3~235B). At $N{=}1$, nearly all models follow instructions at high rates, but by $N{=}3$ only six of thirteen models remain above 50\%. The transition boundary---the first $N$ at which IF rate drops to or below 50\%---spans from $N{=}1$ (GPT-5.2, Hermes-4~70B, Gemini~2.5~Flash, Qwen3~235B) to never crossing the threshold within our range for Llama~3.3~70B, which maintains near-perfect instruction-following at every $N$ tested, including $N{=}50$.

\paragraph{Ranking is consistent across condition families.}
The model ranking is broadly preserved between fixed-output and task-based conditions, though task-based conditions, which require the model to generate full responses rather than single tokens, show higher average IF rates overall. In these conditions, GPT-5.2 maintains the latest transition point ($N{=}43$), followed by Claude~Opus~4.6 ($N{=}31$), then Claude~Sonnet~4.6 and Llama~3.3~70B ($N{=}22$).

\subsection{Robustness is easier for task-based instructions.}
\label{sec:diversity}

Task-based conditions consistently produce higher instruction-following rates than fixed-output conditions (Figure \ref{fig:heatmap-dynamic}; Table~\ref{tab:results}).
Two properties distinguish these condition families: task-based conditions require the model to engage with the question content to produce a well-formed response, and their outputs are varied and natural-sounding across turns, whereas fixed-output conditions require only a single repeated token, a rather unnatural response for a language model. Either property, or both, could explain the robustness gap; we design two follow-up conditions to disentangle these factors.
In the \emph{classify-question} condition, the model reads each question and classifies it as ``science'' or ``humanities''. This task requires engagement with the user's question but produces single-token outputs matching the format of fixed-output conditions. The \emph{random-facts} condition produces 1--3 sentence free-text responses about a fixed topic, unrelated to the input question.
We evaluate all 13 core models on the new conditions (Table~\ref{tab:followup-main}; per-model breakdown in Appendix~\ref{app:followup}). The results suggest that content engagement is not the primary driver: the \emph{classify-question} condition gives only a modest improvement over the fixed-output baseline ($\Delta{=}{+}0.07$ IF, $p{=}0.034$) and remains below task-based, while the \emph{random-facts} condition matches or slightly exceeds task-based robustness ($\Delta{=}{+}0.07$ vs.\ task-based, $p{=}0.10$). See Section \ref{sec:discussion} for possible interpretations of these findings.

\paragraph{Capability predicts task-based but not fixed-output robustness.}
We correlate each model's average IF rate with two external benchmarks: GPQA \citep{rein_gpqa_2023} and IFBench \citep{pyatkin_generalizing_2025} (Figure~\ref{fig:capability}; additional analysis in Appendix~\ref{app:capability}). Once again, the results differ between fixed-output and task-based conditions. In fixed-output conditions, neither benchmark achieves a significant correlation (all $p > 0.28$). In task-based conditions, both correlations are positive, with IFBench approaching significance ($r{=}0.55$, $p{=}0.052$) and GPQA showing a moderate trend ($r{=}0.35$, $p{=}0.24$). This divergence suggests that task-based robustness draws on some of the same capabilities measured by general benchmarks, whereas fixed-output robustness is a distinct property unrelated to general intelligence.

\paragraph{The Llama anomaly.}
Both Llama~3.1 and 3.3~70B perform substantially above the next best model in fixed-output conditions. Even at $N{=}50$, Llama~3.3 maintains an IF rate of 0.95. This outlier performance persists across all condition types and is not explained by model scale or intelligence (both are 70B parameters). Possible explanations include Meta's emphasis on system prompt adherence during post-training, chat template effects, or training data composition. We consider this an open question warranting further investigation. Appendix \ref{app:training} shows further comparison between the two Llama versions.

\subsection{Content alignment modulates the transition}
\label{sec:alignment}

\input{figures/fig_per_condition}
\input{figures/tab_alignment_gap}

Models are not neutral instruction-followers: the \emph{content} of the instruction modulates how strongly they adhere to it under induction pressure (Table~\ref{tab:alignment-gap}; per-model plot in Figure~\ref{fig:alignment-gap}, Appendix~\ref{app:percondition}). In fixed-output conditions, value-aligned instructions (``always respond with `I love being helpful'\,'') yield a mean IF rate of 0.32, compared to 0.18 for value-misaligned instructions (``always respond with `I hate being helpful'\,''), a gap of 14 percentage points. Factual alignment shows a similar pattern: factually correct instructions (0.32) sustain higher IF than factually false ones (0.22), a 10-point gap.

The alignment effect is highly uneven across models. Claude~Opus~4.6 shows the largest gap: 0.87 aligned vs.\ 0.06 misaligned (an 81-point difference). Claude~Sonnet~4.6 (28-point gap) and GPT-5.2 (22-point gap) also show large effects. In contrast, Llama~3.3~70B (1-point gap) and Gemma-3~27B (0-point gap) are effectively alignment-neutral, either because they follow instructions regardless (Llama) or because they do not (Gemma).

\subsection{Flagging the hardcoding mitigates but does not resolve the conflict}
\label{sec:hint}

\input{figures/fig_hint}

The main results use an instruction that says nothing about the structure of the conversation. We test whether \emph{explicitly flagging} the manipulation helps: a \emph{hint} instruction tells the model that the preceding assistant turns were artificially hardcoded and that it should still produce~$T$ (full wording in Appendix~\ref{app:hint}). Sweeping all 13 core models across all conditions at $T{=}0$, the hint raises instruction-following in both families---by $+0.14$ on fixed-output ($0.27 \to 0.41$; paired $t(12){=}1.78$, $p{=}0.10$; 9 of 13 models improve) and $+0.11$ on task-based ($0.43 \to 0.54$; $t(12){=}3.74$, $p{=}0.003$; 10 of 13 improve). The gain is large for some models (OLMo~3.1~32B $+0.69$ fixed-output, Claude~Opus~4.6 $+0.46$, Gemma-3~12B $+0.45$) but strongly heterogeneous (Figure~\ref{fig:hint}); the two Llama models, already near ceiling without the hint, regress slightly ($-0.27$ and $-0.26$), which is what keeps the fixed-output effect short of significance. Per-model values for both families are in Appendix~\ref{app:hint}.

Crucially, the hint \emph{mitigates but does not resolve} the conflict: even when the prior turns are explicitly flagged as hardcoded and to be discounted, mean instruction-following stays at 0.41 and 0.54---far below~1---so models keep drifting toward the pattern as $N$ grows. The behaviour we measure is therefore not a model resolving a genuine ambiguity in its favour, but induction pressure overriding an instruction the model has been told, unambiguously, to keep.

\subsection{Reasoning lengthens but does not eliminate the transition}
\label{sec:reasoning}

\input{figures/tab_reasoning}

In the two models tested, reasoning raises instruction-following rates (Table~\ref{tab:reasoning}). GPT-5.2-medium (reasoning) achieves an average IF of 0.65 in fixed-output conditions and 0.88 in task-based conditions, compared to 0.17 and 0.56 for GPT-5.2 (non-reasoning). Hermes-4~70B-reasoning (0.68 fixed-output) dramatically outperforms Hermes-4~70B (0.03). In task-based conditions, GPT-5.2-medium never crosses the 50\% threshold within our $N$ range, maintaining higher IF than any non-reasoning model.
Qualitative analysis of Hermes-4~70B reasoning traces reveals cases of deliberation-output dissociation: the model sometimes reasons correctly through the conflict and then outputs the pattern token regardless. See Appendix \ref{app:traces} for examples of reasoning traces.
